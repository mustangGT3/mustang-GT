History & Origins

The Ford Mustang was launched in 1964 by the Ford Motor Company. It was introduced to the public at the World's Fair in New York in April 1964 and quickly became an American automotive icon. The Mustang was conceived as an affordable, sporty car aimed at younger buyers and combined performance with distinctive styling.

Where it began

The Mustang was developed at Ford's headquarters in Dearborn, Michigan. It was built on the Ford Falcon platform to keep costs down and allow quick development. Production began at the Ford plant in Dearborn and later expanded to several other plants as demand surged.

Key milestones (timeline)

  • 1964 (April) — First public unveiling at the World's Fair; often referred to as the 1964 1/2 model year.
  • 1965 — The first full model year; immediate sales success and cultural impact.
  • 1967–1968 — First major redesigns, introduction of higher-performance variants.
  • 1969–1970 — Muscle car era peak with powerful V8 engines and Shelby collaborations.
  • 2005 — Retro-inspired redesign brought the classic look back to a modern platform.
  • 2015 — S550 generation introduced independent rear suspension and modernized performance features.
  • 2020s — Expansion into electrified models (Mach-E) and continued high-performance Shelby and GT variants.

Why it mattered

The Mustang created a new car segment, the 'pony car' — affordable sporty coupes with long hoods and short rear decks. It set design and marketing templates other manufacturers copied. Over the decades, the Mustang remained relevant by evolving in technology while honoring its heritage.
